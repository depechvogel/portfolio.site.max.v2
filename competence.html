Development

Development file. This page reflects on where I came from, how I developed during my second year, and what I want to focus on next.

past.txt

Past

Before Industrial Design, my making experience was broad but unstructured. I moved between physical materials, digital tools and unfinished personal experiments. Growing up around games, interfaces, website builders and music software made digital systems feel natural to explore. Before university, I had also experimented with Fusion 360 and Blender, which gave me a wide but still scattered base to build on.

Next to that, I was always interested in science. Physics, math and chemistry felt engaging, while biology and later human physiology were especially intriguing. The way the human body operates still fascinates me. That interest also connected to my roughly three years of working in elderly care, where I saw how much badly designed products and systems can affect people’s independence and frustration in everyday life. Products or systems that seem simple to one person can become difficult when they do not fit someone’s physical ability, routine or experience with technology.

That combination of science, health and making is why I first started at TU/e with Biomedical Engineering. The programme was genuinely interesting and gave me a stronger scientific and evidence-based foundation. However, I quickly started missing the design aspect. I missed sketching, modelling, building and spending time in the workshop. After one year I switched to Industrial Design. Here, my technical curiosity, interest in people and need to create could come together in a way that truly fitted me.

present_overview.txt

Present

I am now in the last quartile of my second year. This year was an opportunity to work on areas I did not have the most experience in yet. I started to focus on specific electives and chose roles within projects and courses that allowed me to work on weaknesses. I also chose to describe my development less as five separate expertise areas and more as a set of competences that connect across them. A prototype is not only Technology & Realisation. It can also be a way to test assumptions, communicate a story, create an aesthetic experience or show value to stakeholders. The four competences below describe the clearest parts of my development this year.

context.txt

Designing from context

Designing from context is about understanding the people, setting and assumptions around a project before deciding what the solution should be. In terms of expertise areas, this sits mainly in User & Society, because it starts from people and context. At the same time, it also touches Business & Entrepreneurship, since stakeholders and organisations shape what a design can realistically become.

In my first year, I mostly connected this to user testing. I saw it as a way to check whether something was understandable, usable or fun. That was already useful in ID-8, where playtesting showed us that the competitive game structure did not fit our goal of collaboration. Because of that, we changed the game into a cooperative design challenge.

In my second year, this became broader. Hart van Woensel was the clearest example. Because we worked with Catharina Hospital and the Woensel neighbourhood, the user was not just one person. It included residents, hospital staff, informal caregivers, local organisations and the hospital itself. Through interviews, field observations, stakeholder mapping and client meetings, we learned that the problem was not only medical. Loneliness, limited support at home, low structure and lack of community connection could all influence pressure on care. That changed the project from a more direct intervention into a low-threshold community concept.

Courses like Intercultural Design and Sustainability and Design also helped me look at users less from my own perspective. They made me more aware that behaviour, values and needs are shaped by culture, environment and social context. The workout app project helped with this as well, because it forced me to think about how different people approach something I personally have a very specific routine and mindset around. My next step here is to become more comfortable staying in the uncertainty of the context for longer. Instead of trying to define the solution too quickly, I want to spend more time understanding what different people actually experience as the problem.

making.txt

Thinking through making

Thinking through making is probably the competence that feels most natural to me. I understand ideas best by sketching, building, testing and adjusting them. In year 1, I mostly saw making as the construction of physical prototypes. Over time, it became more embedded in the full design trajectory. I started using sketches, mock-ups, models and technical experiments not only to finish concepts, but also to explore uncertainty, explain decisions and test assumptions.

This is most directly connected to Technology & Realisation, but for me it never stays there alone. The prototypes I make also involve Math, Data & Computing through code and sensors, and Creativity & Aesthetics through form, material feedback and interaction quality.

My first real step into physical-digital behaviour came during Creative Programming. In Challenge 2, I used Processing and Arduino to build a small rocket game controlled by a potentiometer and thermistor. The setup was simple, but it showed me how code, sensor input and physical control could create a playful digital response.

After that, BOB became a more complete technical prototype. I worked with Arduino, heart rate sensing, servo movement, LEDs and 3D printed components. What interested me most was the chain of actions inside the prototype: measuring heart rate, comparing it to a threshold, and translating that reading into light feedback and a breathing motion. The electronics had to support the intended calming ritual, not just work as separate technical parts.

In year 2, The Bomb Clock pushed this competence into a more refined direction. The goal was not only to make the alarm puzzle function, but to tune how it felt in the hands. The prototype was rebuilt and adjusted almost every week, which made iteration very direct. Small changes in knob resistance, button feedback, material weight and the final red release button changed the tension of the object. That course made me realise that prototyping is also about tuning physical qualities, not only assembling electronics or mechanisms.

evidence.txt

Grounding ideas in evidence

Grounding ideas in evidence is about making design decisions less dependent on assumptions. For me, this connects to data, user research and structured observation. I do not think every design choice can be proven with numbers, but evidence can give a design direction more weight and prevent it from becoming only personal preference.

This competence is closely tied to Math, Data & Computing, but it also supports User & Society and Business & Entrepreneurship. Evidence can come from datasets, sensor measurements, interviews, observations or business analysis.

In year 1, Foundations of Data Analytics was my main introduction to this way of working. We worked with large datasets, cleaned them, searched for relationships and created models from them. That course made me more comfortable with using data to support an argument. It also became useful later in the workout app project, where patterns, variables and user behaviour are central to the logic of the app.

The Exercise & Sleep Quality Study was another important step. My group used Mi Band heart-rate data, an ESP32 with a DHT11 temperature and humidity sensor, smartphone audio recordings and daily questionnaires to study whether endurance exercise before sleep influenced sleep quality. It was limited and messy, but that made it more useful to learn from. I saw that collecting data from real behaviour is fragile: timestamps shift, audio files fail, sensor exports need cleaning, and participant routines do not line up neatly. Sensor values do not automatically mean anything; they need a clear research question, careful interpretation and awareness of limitations.

This year, Design Innovation Methods added a business-oriented layer. We analysed an existing company and created a value proposition based on facts, trends and numbers. This showed me that data can also reveal design opportunities in a market or organisation. Looking at users, competitors and company positioning made the proposal less random and more defendable.

I see this competence as a way to keep my intuition in check. I still like acting quickly and making things, but research and data help me question whether a direction actually makes sense. My next step is to become better at choosing what kind of evidence a project actually needs. Sometimes that means numbers, but sometimes interviews, observations or small tests are more useful than a dataset.

communication.txt

Communicating meaning and value

Communicating meaning and value is something I had to work on quite deliberately this year. I often have enough ideas, sketches and iterations, but in the past I did not always present them in a way that made the reasoning easy to follow. I started to realise that communication is not only the final layer of a project. It shapes how people read the concept, whether they trust it, and whether they understand why it matters.

This competence connects Creativity & Aesthetics with Business & Entrepreneurship. A concept needs visual clarity and a strong identity, but it also needs to communicate why it matters and for whom it creates value.

This became very visible in Hart van Woensel. Because the concept was a community-centred system rather than one physical product, the presentation had to carry a lot of weight. Our coach and Catharina Hospital pushed us to define a clearer identity for the proposal. The booklet, logo, physical model and presentation material were not just there to make the project look finished. They helped communicate accessibility, trust and community. They also made the value of the concept easier to understand for different stakeholders.

My feedback on the previous PI&V assignment also made me look at Business & Entrepreneurship more critically. I had connected it too much to client contact and professional presentation. Those are relevant, but too limited. In Design Innovation Methods, we analysed an existing company and created a value proposition based on facts, trends and numbers. That helped me understand that value has to be argued, not just assumed.

Sustainability and Design added another layer to this. A design proposal can create economic value, but also social and environmental value. That made me more aware that a strong concept should be desirable, responsible and realistic within the system it enters.

For me, this competence is about translating a design into something others can understand and believe in. My next step is to become more precise in explaining what kind of value a concept creates, for whom it creates that value, and what would need to be tested before claiming that value too strongly.

future.txt

Future

For the rest of this year, my main focus is finishing my current courses properly and using them to keep developing the points I mentioned. Intercultural Design helps me look beyond my own assumptions and understand how culture and context influence design. Engineering for People is especially relevant because it is multidisciplinary. Working with students from different programmes gives me the chance to practise being the industrial designer in a team of specialists. PI&V will help me keep reflecting on where I am going as a designer and how I can communicate that more clearly.

Next year, I am planning to do an internship before starting my BEP. I am mainly looking for a place that combines hands-on product development with interaction, health, movement, accessibility or technical prototyping. That direction fits my identity because I want to keep working close to materials, users and technical systems. It also fits my vision, because I am interested in products and systems that make technology feel more understandable and human.

Until now, most of my design work has happened within university projects. An internship would give me a more realistic view of how a design company actually works, what is expected from a designer, and how projects are handled in a professional context. I also want to learn from people with vastly more experience in specific areas, because that is one of the ways I develop fastest.

The internship would also be a good place to work on my main development points: documenting my process more clearly, staying longer in the research and framing phase before moving into making, and explaining the value of a concept for users, clients and organisations. By the time I start my BEP, I want to have a clearer sense of what kind of professional design context fits me best, and how I can use my strengths in that final bachelor project.
