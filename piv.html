professional_identity.txt

Professional identity

I am a designer that thinks through making. I usually need to sketch, build, test or take apart ideas to best understand them. I have been this way for a very long time. Before joining Industrial Design, I was always creating. I made things with wood, metal, my dad’s 3D printer, and a lot of digital tools as well, mostly by figuring things out as I went. After joining Industrial Design, that hands-on way of working became a lot more structured. I still rely heavily on experimentation, but I have learned to connect it to research, user feedback and theoretical design reasoning.

During my second year at TU/e, making became present throughout the whole process. In year 1, I mostly connected it to physical prototypes. This year, I used it earlier and in more ways. In Hart van Woensel, we had to create presentations, mock-ups and models to communicate our research and early design directions to Catharina Hospital. In that project, making became part of the research and communication, not just the final result. In Earth Day Celebration, the capsule, counters, menu and table setting were used to create a situation that people could react to. The Bomb Clock pushed this further by encouraging me to create and iterate something every week. These projects made me realise that making does not only mean producing an object at the end. It can also mean building a story, a test, a question or a shared understanding.

I am not very fixed in the tools I use. Often I have a clear idea of what I want something to become. If I do not yet know the software, technique or material process needed to make it, I try to learn it. This has happened with coding in multiple different languages, working with Arduino, 3D modelling, visual layout, presenting and physical prototyping. At the same time, I understand that a designer does not have to do everything alone. I enjoy working with people who know more than me in specific areas, because it makes the project stronger and pushes me to learn faster. That is one of the reasons I like multidisciplinary CBL projects so much. Different people bring different strengths, and the challenge becomes how to combine those strengths into one coherent design.

What I enjoy most is being present throughout the full process. I do not only want to contribute right at the beginning or end. I like the early confusion of framing a problem, the experimental stage of sketching and testing, the technical process of making something work, and the final step of communicating the idea to stakeholders. The values that keep coming back in my work are curiosity, intentionality, clarity and a strong connection between idea and execution. My current identity as a designer can be defined by that combination: I am a hands-on, technically curious designer who wants to stay close to the full process, from research and framing to prototyping and communication. My strength is not one specific tool, but the ability to move between tools, people and perspectives to make an idea real and understandable.

vision.txt

Vision

My interest in being present throughout the whole design process strongly influences my vision on design as well. Because I think through making, I value designs where the process behind the outcome is still visible. I think good design should feel intentional. A product should not only work well or look finished; it should have a clear reason for why it exists, why it looks the way it does, and why someone would want to use it. That reason is not something you add at the end. It is built through research, material choices, testing, technical decisions, mistakes and lots of iterations.

This has become more important to me because of how quickly design outcomes can now be produced. With generative AI, image tools and fast digital workflows, it is becoming easier to make something that looks polished at first glance. I do not think that is necessarily a bad thing. I use digital tools myself, and I think designers should stay open to new technologies. However, when polished output becomes easier to make, the process behind it becomes way more important. A clean render is not enough if there is no real reasoning, context or story behind it.

I also became more aware this year that good design depends heavily on perspective. Second-year courses I followed, like Sustainability and Design and Intercultural Design, focused a lot on looking at problems from different cultural, social and environmental viewpoints. The workout app project helped with this as well, because it forced me to think about how different people approach something I personally have a very specific routine and mindset around. These experiences made me realise that what feels logical and intuitive to me is not automatically the same for someone else. My experience in elderly care already showed me how frustrating products can become for people when they are designed from a distance. These courses pushed that idea further by making me think more about the assumptions I bring into a project.

That is why I still believe design should be intuitive and accessible, but I now connect that more strongly to authenticity and storytelling. Good design should bring technology back to a human scale. It should not hide behind complexity. The kind of design I want to move toward is physical, understandable and meaningful: shaped by real context, real testing and a clear design intention. In the future, I want to contribute to products and systems, especially in areas like health, movement, accessibility or physical-digital interaction, that make technology feel more human instead of more distant.
